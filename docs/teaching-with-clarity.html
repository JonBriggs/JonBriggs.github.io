Teaching with Clarity in the Age of AI

essay · working draft

When I played hockey, ice time was precious. Our coach — who was also my academic advisor — ran practice on a minute-by-minute agenda, and at the end of every session came the wind sprints.1 Coach John was clear about their purpose: the only point of sprinting was to increase our own capacity, our speed and our endurance. And here was the thing he couldn't do anything about. He could set the drill, blow the whistle, and watch us skate. He could not feel whether any one of us had actually reached the edge of our effort. Only the skater knew that.

1 The wind sprint has stayed with me for years as the truest picture I have of learning: effort at the edge of capacity, where discomfort is evidence, not failure.

I've come to think teaching has the same structure, and that artificial intelligence has thrown it into sharp relief.

Most of what teachers respond to are proxies. We see the finished problem set, the essay, the confident answer in discussion — products and performances that stand in for the learning we can't observe directly.2 Good teachers get remarkably skilled at reading those signals. But the signal was never the thing itself. Whether a student genuinely struggled, reached for something just beyond their grasp, and built capacity in the reaching — that's known fully only by the one instrument none of us can see from the outside: the learner's own sense of whether their brain had to sweat.

2 Once you start to see them, proxies are everywhere — grades, completion, fluency, the confident voice in seminar. None of them is learning. All of them get mistaken for it.

For a long time the proxies were reliable enough. A polished essay usually meant a mind had done polished work. AI loosens that link. When a machine can produce a passable version of almost any product we used to assign, the product tells us less about the person who handed it in. The wind sprint can now be skated by something other than the student.

It would be easy to read that as a threat, and plenty of the conversation does. The more useful reading, I think, is that AI has made an old problem unavoidable. It hasn't changed the purpose of school; it has made clarity about that purpose more important. We can no longer coast on proxies. We have to be able to say what good learning actually looks like, where the effort needs to land, and how we'll know — or honestly admit when we can't.

The risk isn't that students will struggle too much. It's that they'll struggle too little, and mistake fluency for understanding.

There's a second effect worth naming. AI dramatically expands the possibility space around any problem: more directions, more drafts, more plausible answers, faster.3 That abundance is genuinely useful, and it carries a temptation — to commit early, take the first coherent option the machine offers, and stop thinking. Dunning and Kruger described that gap long before any of this; AI just makes it cheaper to fall into.

3 Csikszentmihalyi's flow is the better picture of the target: a zone where challenge and capacity are matched — hard enough to demand full effort, not so hard it collapses into despair.

So part of the work, as I see it, is designing for productive uncertainty — building conditions where ambiguity stays open long enough for real thinking to happen. Not confusion for its own sake. The teacher's craft is increasingly about holding that zone open when the easiest path is to let the machine close it.

What does teaching with clarity ask of us, concretely? It asks us to be explicit about which assignments are products and which are practice — which wind sprints actually build capacity. It asks us to make the inner instrument visible: to help students notice and name when they're genuinely working versus when they're being carried. And it asks a kind of intellectual humility of us too, because the honest answer to "did learning happen here?" is often "I designed for it, and I can't fully see it."

None of this is a tidy solution, and I'm wary of anyone who offers one. What I'm confident in is the orientation. Not because we have all the answers about AI and learning, but because asking these questions clearly — together, as a faculty and a community — matters more than racing to a policy that pretends the questions are settled. The wind sprints still matter. We just have to be clearer than ever about why we're running them, and honest that, in the end, only the runner knows.
