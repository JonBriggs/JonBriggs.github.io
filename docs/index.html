I work at the seam between how a school runs and what it teaches, and I write about how instruction changes when the tools change. This is where the thinking lives.

Most conversations about AI in schools begin with a question about cheating. It's the obvious place to start, and it's the wrong one. The harder question is quieter: what happens to learning when the struggle that produces it becomes optional?1

1 I've taken to calling this cognitive surrender — distinct from academic dishonesty, and considerably harder to see.

There's a distinction to be made between a tool that extends what a student can do and one that quietly replaces the part where the learning happens. A calculator did the former for arithmetic we'd already internalized. The current tools do something less settled, because they arrive before the internalizing is finished.

What schools have always offered, underneath the curriculum, is a place where that internalizing is supervised by people who know the student.2 That doesn't disappear in an AI-present world. If anything it becomes the scarce thing — the part no tool supplies on its own.

2 The operational bottleneck was never the teaching. It was attention at scale: no one reads four years of a hundred students' work. That constraint is the one actually shifting — and the one I tend to build against.
The question isn't whether students will use these tools. It's whether they'll know when they care about thinking for themselves — and that is a skill, not an instinct.

So the work ahead is less about policy than about design: building the conditions under which a student chooses the struggle, and the means by which a school can actually see them becoming more themselves. I do that from two directions — writing about it, and building the tools that test whether the thinking holds. Neither is solved. Both are worth the attention.
